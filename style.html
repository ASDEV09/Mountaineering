Alpine Style

Alpine climbing involves advanced skills like rock, ice, and mixed climbing to summit large routes in mountainous areas. Climbers move in small teams, carrying their own equipment, facing risks like rockfalls, avalanches, and altitude effects. The "golden age" saw pioneers like Walter Bonatti in the Alps, followed by ascents on major peaks like the Himalayas and Patagonia in "alpine style." Notable alpinists include Reinhold Messner and Ueli Steck. Piolets d'Or awards achievements in alpine climbing.

Expedition Style

Expedition style, also known as "siege" style, involves setting up fixed camps and stocked supply lines on a mountain, allowing climbers to ascend at their own pace. It utilizes fixed ropes and multiple trips to establish camps and secure the route. In contrast, Alpine style requires climbers to carry all their supplies and climb the route in a single continuous push, without fixed ropes or established camps. Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay used expedition style in the first ascent of Mount Everest.

Bouldering

Bouldering is a form of free climbing done on small rock formations or artificial walls without ropes or harnesses. Climbers typically use climbing shoes, chalk, and mats to enhance grip and safety. Bouldering problems, sequences of moves to complete a climb, are usually under 6 meters tall. Traverses involve climbing horizontally. Originally a training method for mountaineering, it evolved into a separate discipline. Bouldering problems are rated for difficulty using systems like the V-scale or Fontainebleau scale. Its popularity has raised environmental concerns due to soil erosion, leading to access restrictions in some areas.

Free Climbing

Free climbing is a rock climbing style where climbers ascend a route using only their hands, feet, and body, without ropes or artificial aids. In this challenging technique, climbers rely solely on their physical and technical skills to navigate the terrain and reach the top of the climb. Unlike aid climbing, where climbers use equipment to assist their ascent, free climbing emphasizes the natural movement and strength of the climber, making it a physically demanding and skill-intensive discipline.

Ice Climbing

Ice climbing is a specialized discipline where climbers ascend frozen water routes using double ice axes, crampons, and steel ice screws for protection. Routes include frozen waterfalls, alpine couloirs, and hanging icicles. Grades peak at WI6 to WI7, with WI7 being rare and extremely challenging. Mixed climbing combines ice and rock, pushing technical difficulty. UIAA regulates ice climbing competitions in lead and speed formats. Helmcken Falls in Canada offers severely overhanging bolted ice climbing routes graded up to WI13, considered some of the world's hardest ice climbs.

Traditional Climbing

Traditional climbing, or trad climbing, is a form of free climbing where the lead climber places protection equipment while ascending the route. The second climber removes this gear as they climb. Unlike sport climbing, where bolts are pre-drilled into the rock, trad climbing carries higher risk due to potential incorrect placement of protection or limited opportunities for insertion on challenging routes. Though once dominant, trad climbing popularity declined in favor of sport climbing since the mid-1980s.

Sport Climbing

Sport climbing, also known as bolted climbing, involves climbers clipping into pre-drilled bolts on the rock face for protection while ascending a route. It is safer than traditional climbing, where climbers insert temporary protection equipment. Sport climbing emerged in the early 1980s when climbers sought routes without natural cracks for traditional protection. Despite controversy, it gained popularity and led to the growth of competition climbing. This discipline made its Olympic debut at the 2020 Summer Olympics, comprising lead climbing, bouldering, and speed climbing, although the term "sport climbing" can be confusingly used to refer to competition climbing.

Aid Climbing

Aid climbing is a rock climbing technique using mechanical devices like aiders for upward progress, opposite of free climbing. Traditional aid climbing involves hammering fixed pitons and bolts, while clean aid climbing uses removable placements. Its origins date back to early climbing with ladders and pitons. In the 1960s-1970s, it thrived on Yosemite's big walls. The grading system shifted from A to C-grade due to evolving techniques. Aid climbing is still used on challenging big wall and alpine routes, helping climbers overcome extreme difficulties beyond free climbing capabilities.
